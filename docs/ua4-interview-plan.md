## Open-ended phase guide

Question areas for the problem-discovery half of the interview. Ordered roughly broad to specific. Two or three example phrasings per area. **Bullet points, not a script.**

> Reminders:
>
> - Ask about past behavior, not hypothetical future behavior. "When was the last time…" beats "would you ever…".
> - Don't ask hypothetical, feature, or validation questions. They produce nodding, not learning.
> - Don't pitch your idea in this phase.
> - Let silence be okay.

- **A specific past moment of struggle**
  - "What's a time in the past where you felt like you were struggling to keep up with school, sports, employment, etc.?"
  - "Walk me through the last time everything felt like too much at once."

- **What they did when it hit**
  - "What did you do when the struggle hit?"
  - "How did you handle it in that moment?"

- **How long it lasted**
  - "For how long did you struggle with that?"
  - "Was that a one-time thing or does it come back?"

- **What specifically was overwhelming**
  - "During that time, what specifically felt most overwhelming?"
  - "What things were piling up on you?"

- **Their current approach / workaround**
  - "What things have you done to help with the struggle?"
  - "What does your system look like for keeping track of everything — or do you have one?"

- **Whether they've looked for solutions**
  - "What has worked in the past and what hasn't?"
  - "Have you ever looked for an app or tool to help you manage it all?"

## Solution feedback phase guide

Only entered after the user has confirmed the problem in their own words. If they have not, skip this phase — that interview was about confirming the problem.

- **What to show, in what order:**
  - First: the first draft of the website / visual concept — no explanation, just put it in front of them
  - Introduce it with: "I've been working on something — I'll show it to you and just tell me what you think it does."
- **What to listen for:**
  - Where they get confused or hesitate — that's more valuable than "this is cool"
  - Whether they immediately understand what it does without you explaining
  - Whether they connect it to the struggle they just described
  - Moments where they start interacting with it differently than expected
- **Prompts:**
  - "Tell me what you think this does."
  - "Show me how you'd actually use this."
  - "Walk me through what you'd do next here."
  - "Does this connect to anything you described earlier?"

- "Would it be okay to come back with a follow-up question if something comes up?"

### Interview 3 — Ruben (date TBD)

#### Need or opportunity

- **Current belief:** Teenagers struggle to balance their academic workload with everything else competing for their time.
- **Confirms it if:** Ruben mentions needing to cut sleep, or that he starts slacking academically or in sports when both peak at the same time.
- **Refutes it if:** Ruben says he manages everything well and doesn't feel the struggle.
- **Complicates it if:** Ruben says the struggle only hits for short periods during the year.
- **What actually happened:** Confirmed and complicated. XC season ends when fall semester gets hardest; track ends when spring semester peaks. Uses all available time in school to compensate, strictly sleeps at 10pm. Stresses his body and mind significantly. Struggle lasts about 2 weeks each time. Complication: the problem is time-limited (2 weeks, twice a year) but real and painful when it hits. Clause holds.

#### Competitive alternative

- **Current belief:** Teenagers rely on memory, alarms, or basic planner apps to manage their workload.
- **Confirms it if:** Ruben explicitly says he uses memory or alarms, or mentions forgetting assignments.
- **Refutes it if:** Ruben says he uses a dedicated planner app consistently.
- **Complicates it if:** Ruben uses planner apps but inconsistently.
- **What actually happened:** Confirmed. Tried using a calendar but abandoned it — found it too complicated and a waste of time. Now just pushes through on his own. Key signal: Study AI must be simpler and faster than a calendar or it will face the same rejection.
