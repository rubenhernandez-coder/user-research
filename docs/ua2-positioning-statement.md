# UA2 — Positioning Statement


The deliverable of Stage 1. Drafted in Pass 1 from current belief, revised across Passes 2–4 as research and interview evidence comes in. Every clause must be defensible by the time Stage 1 exits.

The statement at the top is the public-facing artifact — short, clean, one paragraph. The evidence record below it is the working document where the student's thinking lives. The messiness in the evidence record is the point. Cleaning it up to look polished destroys the record of learning. This is a living document; its git history is part of the deliverable.

---

## Statement

> For teenagers juggling academics and extracurricular obligations, who struggle to balance their academic workload with everything else competing for their time, Study AI is a smart study planner that turns a chaotic schedule into a clear plan, reducing the stress of juggling academics and outside commitments. Unlike trying to remember every deadline in your head, setting it as an alarm, or basic planner apps, Study AI does the planning for you, organizes it, and reminds you when to work — so you don't have to think about the system, just the work.

## Evidence

One subsection per clause. Updated continuously across all five passes.

### Target customer

- **Current belief:** Teenagers juggling academics and extracurricular obligations.
- **Basis for the belief:** Ruben's own experience as a student in this age range, plus reasoning that students with outside commitments (sports, jobs, family responsibilities) feel the most acute time pressure.
- **Evidence found:**
  - [none yet — to be confirmed through research and interviews]
- **Alternatives considered:** "Student-athletes" — too narrow, excludes students with jobs or family responsibilities. "Students with a lot of responsibilities" — too vague. "Ages 14–21" — too wide, college students live a different life than high schoolers.
- **What would change my mind:** If interviews reveal that college students or working adults feel this problem more acutely than high schoolers, the target age range may need to shift.

### Need or opportunity

- **Current belief:** They struggle to balance their academic workload with everything else competing for their time.
- **Basis for the belief:** Ruben's own experience; reasoning that students with packed schedules don't lack motivation, they lack a system for fitting everything in.
- **Evidence found:**
  - [none yet — to be confirmed through research and interviews]
- **Alternatives considered:** "High levels of stress" — real but a symptom, not the root problem. "Not using their time efficiently" — accurate but too abstract.
- **What would change my mind:** If interviews reveal the main problem is forgetting deadlines rather than not knowing when to work, the need clause would shift toward memory/reminders.

### Product category

- **Current belief:** Smart study planner
- **Basis for the belief:** "Study planner" is the search term the target user would type. "Smart" signals the app does the planning for you rather than just holding information you enter yourself.
- **Evidence found:**
  - [none yet]
- **Alternatives considered:** "Study planner" alone — undersells what the app does, sounds passive. "Scheduling app" — too generic. "AI-powered study planner" — accurate but wordy.
- **What would change my mind:** If users don't identify as needing a "study planner" but instead search for "time management app" or "productivity app," the category label should shift.

### Key benefit

- **Current belief:** Turns a chaotic schedule into a clear plan, reducing the stress of juggling academics and outside commitments.
- **Basis for the belief:** Reasoning that the core pain is feeling overwhelmed and out of control; a clear plan directly addresses that feeling.
- **Evidence found:**
  - [none yet]
- **Alternatives considered:** "Better academic performance" — real but downstream, not what the user feels immediately. "More focused" — vague. "Help students keep up with their responsibilities" — too flat.
- **What would change my mind:** If interviews show students care more about grades or performance outcomes than stress reduction, the benefit clause should shift.

### Primary competitive alternative

- **Current belief:** Trying to remember every deadline in your head or setting it as an alarm.
- **Basis for the belief:** Ruben's own behavior and observation; most teenagers don't use structured planning tools consistently — they rely on memory and basic reminders.
- **Evidence found:**
  - [none yet]
- **Alternatives considered:** Google Calendar — used by some but not the majority of the target age group. Notes app — possible, but more passive than Study AI's planning function. "Doing nothing" — real but hard to name in a statement.
- **What would change my mind:** If interviews show most target students are already using a structured planning app, the competitive alternative needs to shift to that app specifically.

### Primary differentiation

- **Current belief:** Study AI does the planning for you, organizes it, and reminds you when to work.
- **Basis for the belief:** Alarms and memory tell you what's due; Study AI does the planning for you. The core gap is that every existing tool either requires too much setup before it helps (Google Calendar, structured planners) or gives no guidance at all (paper, memory). Study AI eliminates the setup burden and does the thinking for the student.
- **Evidence found:**
  - Ruben (interview): Tried using a calendar but abandoned it — found it too complicated and a waste of time. Now just pushes through on his own.
  - Jayden (interview): Wasn't using any planning tool at all. His coping strategy was brute force and asking for extensions.
  - Reddit reviews (secondary research): Recurring theme across multiple reviewers — anything requiring setup or maintenance gets abandoned. One reviewer said their brain "refuses to use anything premade." Another said a phone calendar with auto-reminders is "the only way anything ever gets done." The myHomework review showed a beloved app lost its users entirely when it added complexity.
  - Synthesis: The differentiation is not just "tells you when to work" — it's that Study AI requires almost no setup and does the planning automatically, so students who abandoned every other tool have a reason to stick with this one.
- **Alternatives considered:** "Won't forget" — true but an alarm does this too, not unique enough. "Structured plan ahead of the deadline" — real but doesn't capture the low-friction angle. "Visualization of available time" — too abstract, doesn't communicate the automatic planning aspect.
- **What would change my mind:** If a competing product already does low-friction automatic planning well and has significant adoption among the target group, the differentiation needs to be sharpened further. Still needs direct interview confirmation to move to evidenced by interview.
